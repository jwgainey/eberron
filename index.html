Eberron - Important Info

Eberron is a dungeon-punk setting. This means it is a fusion of a more modern, gritty setting of noir and the heroic fantasy genres. A big component of this is the presence of magitech, while fantasy settings have had this incorporated fairly commonly, instances of its use were either rare or something from an ‘ancient civilization’. In this world technology is fueled and innovated by magic and is rather commonplace. For instance, the Lightning Rail is a train like we have, except it is powered by a bound lighting elemental instead of steam or batteries. There are street lights made from permanent Dancing Lights, wash stations made from lode stones enchanted with prestidigitation and magic infused robots, the Warforged. The setting I’m making is based in a rough technological equivalence of the 20s with much of the magitech being fairly new. So while in other settings magic can easily replace tech, like Sending or Message instead of radio or cell phones, items are in use much, much more commonly used than casting spells. The setting could be considered fairly low fantasy due to the lack of mages running around. Other than a literal handful of powerful caster NPCs in the world, most traditional spellcasting will be done by you guys. Any other casters will function as very low level casters at best and typically rely on ritual magic when any is actually in use. This still goes along with the themes of magic item crafting being more commonplace. That said, magic items, like clothing that changes color or styles, pens that don’t run out of ink, and illusions as a part of theatrics are part of everyday use for the majority of people.

Religion functions a little differently here too. In most other settings the gods are a fact, they regularly directly or indirectly interact with the world and channel their powers through clerics, druids and warlocks. Here, it’s a little more ambiguous, not that your characters would know, or really be directly affected by. There are pantheons and various creation myths, but who’s right? No one knows for sure (except me). The gods arose from ancient tales, philosophies, and on occasion interaction with outside forces, but these were by no means divine forces. Divine magic here is powered by the user's faith in something more than through direct intervention. Although druids can still be powered by nature spirits if that’s what the player wants. Also the pantheon is entirely different. None of the traditional deities return here beyond small references like spells or a couple constellations. There are a few physical manifestations of divine beings, but they are definitely not the norm. These can be seen in the Silver Flame, a spot where a paladin and coatl sacrificed themselves sealing away a demon and the Undying Court, a group of positive energy powered undead Elves.

Although we usually don’t keep track of alignments anyway, this is actually the first setting where they are more of a suggestion than a rule. Every creature's alignment is based entirely on the choices it makes instead of simply what it is. Another thing to keep in mind is that knowing a language works a little differently. You base the languages you know on where you’re from instead of your race. You could end up with a dwarf that doesn’t know Dwarvish or a human whose first language was Goblin. For a good chunk of the world Common is a second or even third language, it was developed as a trade language with only a few countries eventually adopting it as the official language. Fairly minor in the grand scheme of things, just something to keep in mind.
